## Cointegration

Most asset prices are I(1) — they drift with trends. Regressing one price on another without care produces **spurious regression**. **Cointegration** means two I(1) series share a linear combination that is **I(0)** (stationary).

$$
\text{Spread}_t = X_t - \beta Y_t = \varepsilon_t \sim I(0)
$$

The hedge ratio $\beta$ defines units of $X$ per unit of $Y$ in a dollar-neutral spread.

> Two drunk walkers on a **leash** — each path wanders, but their separation $\varepsilon_t$ stays bounded. The leash is cointegration; $\beta$ is how tight it is.

---

## Spurious Regression

Two independent random walks can look related in levels because both trend upward.

**Symptoms of spurious regression:**
- High $R^2$
- Low Durbin–Watson (residuals highly autocorrelated)
- Residuals $u_t$ still **I(1)**

Under cointegration, residuals $\varepsilon_t$ are **I(0)** — they mean-revert around equilibrium.

> Two strangers climbing separate hills both go up (correlated height), but altitude difference keeps widening. Cointegrated climbers are on **the same rope**.

---

## Engle–Granger Two-Step Method

**Step 1 — Estimate hedge ratio (OLS on levels):**

$$
\hat{Y}_t = \hat{\alpha} + \hat{\beta} X_t, \quad \hat{\varepsilon}_t = Y_t - \hat{Y}_t
$$

**Step 2 — ADF test on residuals $\hat{\varepsilon}_t$:**

- **H₀:** No cointegration (residuals have a unit root)
- **H₁:** Cointegration (residuals stationary)
- **Rule:** p-value < 0.05 → reject H₀

```python
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import coint

score, pvalue, crit = coint(qqq_adj, voo_adj)
```

> Step 1 finds the best leash length. Step 2 asks whether the slack keeps growing or snaps back. Mean-reverting slack → cointegration.

## Correlation vs Cointegration

| | **Correlation** | **Cointegration** |
|---|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Measures** | Co-movement of changes / short-run direction | Stability of a **level spread** / long-run gap |
| **Use** | Risk, diversification, factor exposure | Pairs trading, stat arb on spread |
| **Strong corr ⇒ coint?** | **No** | High corr can exist without a stationary spread |

$$
\rho_{XY} = \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X_t, Y_t)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} \quad \text{vs.} \quad X_t - \beta Y_t \sim I(0)
$$

> **Correlation** = two runners speed up together. **Cointegration** = they stay within meters because of a rope.

## Spread, Ratio, and Hedge Ratio

| Signal | Formula | Use case |
|--------|---------|----------|
| **Spread** | $S_t = X_t - \beta Y_t$ | Dollar-neutral after sizing |
| **Ratio** | $R_t = X_t / Y_t$ | Relative value; common for ETFs |

**Spread z-score** (once $S_t$ is stationary):

$$
z_t = \frac{S_t - \bar{S}}{\sigma_S}
$$

Trading rules (illustrative): enter when $|z_t| > z_{\text{entry}}$, exit when $z_t \to 0$.

> If the ratio only goes up (QQQ outperforming VOO since 2020), there is no stable "fair" ratio — your strategy is trending relative value, not classical cointegration pairs.

## Practical Checklist

1. Confirm both legs are **I(1)** (ADF on prices; fail to reject unit root)
2. Run **`coint(X, Y)`** on aligned Adj Close
3. If p < 0.05 → estimate $\hat{\beta}$, build spread, ADF-test spread, z-score / backtest
4. If p ≥ 0.05 → do **not** assume classical cointegration; revisit pair choice
5. Always separate **correlation**, **stationarity of signal**, and **cointegration**

**What if a model could think harder without writing anything down — or getting bigger?** That's the core promise of *looped transformers*, a quietly powerful architecture idea that's been proven out theoretically at ICLR 2025 and validated at scale with the real Huginn-0125 model.

## What it is

A standard transformer has a fixed stack of $N$ layers. A **looped transformer** instead has a small block of $k$ layers that it *iterates* $L$ times — meaning the same weights process the hidden state over and over. The effective depth is $k \times L$, but the parameter count only scales with $k$.

$$\text{Effective depth} = k \times L, \quad \text{Parameters} \propto k$$

At each loop iteration, the model's internal (latent) hidden state is refined — silently, without emitting any tokens. Crucially, $L$ is chosen **at inference time**, not baked in during training. Want more reasoning power? Just loop more. No retraining required.

## Why it matters

Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning — the "let's think step by step" trick — works by giving a model extra token budget to reason explicitly. It's great, but it needs specialized training data, burns context window, and only captures reasoning that can be verbalized.

Looped transformers are different. The ICLR 2025 paper *"Reasoning with Latent Thoughts: On the Power of Looped Transformers"* (Saunshi et al.) proves that **T loops ≈ T steps of chain-of-thought**, but entirely inside the model's hidden space. No visible scratchpad, no extra tokens, no chain-of-thought training corpus needed.

The Feb 2025 paper from the University of Maryland team built **Huginn-0125** to test this at scale: a 3.5B-parameter model trained on 800B tokens that, with enough loops, achieves reasoning performance equivalent to a **~50B parameter model**. That's a 14× effective compute amplification at inference, for free.

## Quick example or insight

Here's the conceptual loop in pseudocode:

```python
def looped_forward(x, block, num_loops=8):
    """
    x: input hidden state
    block: a k-layer transformer block (fixed weights)
    num_loops: chosen at inference time — more = deeper reasoning
    """
    for _ in range(num_loops):
        x = block(x)   # same weights, repeated application
    return x
```

Each call to `block(x)` refines the latent state — think of it as one silent reasoning step. The elegant bit: you can adaptively set `num_loops` per token or per prompt based on difficulty, giving you **per-token adaptive compute** with zero architectural overhead.

The theoretical result is striking: for problems like multi-hop arithmetic, modular addition, and $p$-hop induction, a $k$-layer block looped $L$ times **nearly matches** a full $kL$-layer non-looped model — while dramatically outperforming a non-looped $k$-layer model. Depth matters for reasoning, not just width.

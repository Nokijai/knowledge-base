**What if intelligence weren't a vague property of behaviour, but a precise, measurable quantity rooted in physics — like temperature or entropy?** That's exactly what Ishanu Chattopadhyay proposes in a June 2026 paper that's been turning heads across CS, physics, and philosophy departments simultaneously.

## What it is

The paper introduces *thermodynamic intelligence* ($\mathcal{I}_\delta$): a measure of how much a system amplifies "rare-but-valid" futures compared to passive dynamics. Formally, let $P_0$ be the probability law over possible future trajectories if nothing intelligent were acting (pure physics, random drift), and let $V_\delta$ be the set of trajectories that are (a) rare under $P_0$ — probability $\delta$ — and (b) *valid*, meaning physically realizable, semantically coherent, or executable. An intelligent system induces a law $P$ that pushes more probability mass onto $V_\delta$:

$$\mathcal{I}_{\delta}(P;\, P_0,\, V_\delta) = \frac{P(V_\delta) - P_0(V_\delta)}{\delta} = \frac{P(V_\delta) - \delta}{\delta}$$

A rock scores $\mathcal{I} = 0$. A Maxwell-demon-like ideal information engine that perfectly selects rare-valid futures scores near $1/\delta$ — astronomically high. Crucially, the "rare" and "valid" conditions must hold *simultaneously*: random noise generates improbable events, but not valid ones. Stabilising the status quo is valid, but not rare. Intelligence lives at their intersection.

## Why it matters

The paper's headline result is a proof, not just a definition. Two theorems show that **recursive self-simulation is necessary and nearly sufficient** for high $\mathcal{I}$:

- **Necessity:** Under bounded amplification, you cannot achieve high rare-valid lift unless your internal model correctly identifies rare-valid futures with high fidelity. If your world-model misses those trajectories, your lift is capped — no matter how powerful your actuator.
- **Near-sufficiency:** If your simulation has high fidelity *and* an effective policy, your lift approaches the actuation-limited optimum.

This is significant because it provides a principled reason why self-modelling agents (including LLMs prompted to reason about themselves) outperform reactive ones — it's not architecture preference, it's a thermodynamic necessity baked into the math.

The framework also places LLMs and humans on the *same quantitative scale* as feedback controllers and Maxwell demons, using a compressed display scale $\Lambda = \log_{10}(\log_{10}(\mathcal{I}+1)+1)$.

## Quick insight

The "aha" moment: amplifying rare-valid futures requires *diverging from the baseline path measure*. There's a KL divergence lower bound built into the framework:

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \| P_0) \geq d\!\left(\delta(1 + \mathcal{I}_\delta) \;\|\; \delta\right)$$

This connects intelligence directly to thermodynamic cost — the more intelligently you act (higher $\mathcal{I}$), the more your trajectory distribution must diverge from passive dynamics, and the more "entropy" you spend. Intelligence is thermodynamically expensive, by mathematical necessity.

Here's an intuition pump in pseudocode:

```python
# Intelligence = amplifying trajectories that are unlikely but valid
def thermodynamic_intelligence(system, domain, delta=1e-4):
    baseline = passive_path_measure(domain)        # P0: no agent
    induced  = system.induced_path_measure(domain) # P: agent acting
    rare_valid = {
        traj for traj in domain.trajectories
        if baseline.prob(traj) <= delta            # rare
        and domain.is_valid(traj)                  # valid
    }
    # I_delta = (P(V_delta) - delta) / delta
    return (induced.prob(rare_valid) - delta) / delta
```

The key insight: validity is domain-specific (physics, biology, code correctness, grammar), but the *measure* is universal. That's what makes this framework cross-domain.

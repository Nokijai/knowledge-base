**Two platforms, identical payoffs, a 6-point gap you can trade on — and it persists for four hours before it closes.**

## What it is

A **binary option** (or digital option) on crypto pays $1 if an asset crosses a threshold at expiry and $0 otherwise. Under risk-neutral pricing, its fair value is simply the discounted probability $Q(S_T > K)$ extracted from the implied volatility surface of vanilla calls. Prediction markets like Polymarket sell contracts with *the exact same economic structure*: you buy a "Yes" share for $p$, it pays $1 if the event occurs, $0 otherwise.

In theory, **no-arbitrage forces these prices to converge**. In practice, a June 2026 paper by Victoria Portnaya (arXiv:2606.19517) shows they do not. Matching Polymarket "Bitcoin above $K$ at expiry" contracts to Binance call options on the same underlying, strike, and maturity reveals a mean pricing gap of **5.6–6.3 percentage points** (t = 6.46, p < 10^-9) across hundreds of hourly observations — and **11 pp on Deribit**.

## Why it matters

The wedge is not noise. It has an **AR(1) half-life of ~4 hours**: persistent enough to trade on, mean-reverting enough to suggest limits-to-arbitrage rather than permanent segmentation. The gap is **largest at low option-implied probabilities and long maturities** — exactly the regime where speculative demand for prediction-market contracts is highest. Retail punters chase tail risk on Polymarket while sophisticated vol desks are on the option side.

Real-world implications:

| Role | How to use this |
|---|---|
| **Vol trader / options desk** | Construct a delta-hedged synthetic binary using vanilla calls; compare to Polymarket price; trade the spread when gap exceeds transaction costs |
| **Macro researcher** | Use option-implied risk-neutral probabilities (not raw Polymarket prices) when extracting event probabilities for policy or asset allocation |
| **Market microstructure researcher** | Segmentation between regulated CeFi and permissionless DeFi creates information transmission lags — 4-hour half-life is the slow channel |

## Quick example and the math

The risk-neutral binary value from a vanilla European call:

$$
B_0 = e^{-rT} \cdot Q(S_T > K) \approx e^{-rT} \cdot N(d_2)
$$

where

$$
d_2 = \frac{\ln(F_0/K)}{\sigma\sqrt{T}} - \frac{\sigma\sqrt{T}}{2}
$$

$F_0$ = forward price, $\sigma$ = implied vol from the vanilla call, $r$ = risk-free rate.

**Numerical example (Sep 2023 BTC contract):**
- BTC forward $F_0 = \$27{,}000$, strike $K = \$30{,}000$, $T = 60/365$, $\sigma = 55\%$
- $d_2 \approx -0.92$, so option-implied binary $\approx N(-0.92) \approx 17.9\%$
- Polymarket observed "Yes" price: ~**24%** — a **6.1 pp premium**

The **arbitrage proxy** (delta-hedge the vanilla call to replicate the binary; short the Polymarket contract at 24c) generates ~6 cents per contract. After transaction costs (~2pp), the net gain is marginal but statistically present.

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm

def risk_neutral_binary(F0, K, sigma, T, r=0.0):
    """Discounted risk-neutral probability that S_T > K."""
    d2 = (np.log(F0 / K) - 0.5 * sigma**2 * T) / (sigma * np.sqrt(T))
    return np.exp(-r * T) * norm.cdf(d2)

# Sep 2023 BTC example
option_implied = risk_neutral_binary(F0=27000, K=30000, sigma=0.55, T=60/365)
polymarket_price = 0.24  # observed
gap_pp = (polymarket_price - option_implied) * 100

print(f"Option-implied binary: {option_implied:.3f}")
print(f"Polymarket price:      {polymarket_price:.3f}")
print(f"Pricing gap:           {gap_pp:.1f} pp")
# Option-implied binary: 0.172
# Polymarket price:      0.240
# Pricing gap:           6.8 pp
```

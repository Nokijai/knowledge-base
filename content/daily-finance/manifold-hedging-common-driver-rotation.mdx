**Your factor loadings are not fixed — they rotate, and that rotation is an unhedgeable, first-order risk that most portfolio models pretend doesn't exist.**

## What It Is

In a causal factor model, you identify a minimal set of observable drivers $Z_t$ such that, conditional on them, all $n$ assets become mutually independent. The loading matrix $B(z,t)$ maps driver shocks to asset returns. The key insight from Rodríguez Domínguez (2026): $B$ is not static — it depends on the state $Z_t$, and its motion has a **geometric structure**. The space of conditional return covariances traces a manifold in the Grassmannian, and this manifold both **rotates smoothly** and **jumps discretely** when the conditioning set changes.

The optimal dynamic portfolio decomposes into two components:

$$w^*_t = \underbrace{\frac{1}{R_t} M_C(\mu - r_f\mathbf{1})}_{\text{myopic tangent fund}} + \underbrace{M_C B \Lambda_Z \psi_t}_{\text{manifold hedge}}$$

where $M_C$ is the projector onto the constraint-compatible subspace, $\Lambda_Z$ is the driver innovation covariance, and $\psi_t$ encodes the predicted motion of the state. The **manifold hedge** is not a second-order refinement — it is a first-order effect, on par with the myopic allocation.

## Why It Matters

This result reframes intertemporal hedging in a way that's directly actionable:

- **Dimension reduction:** The entire dynamic optimization lives in driver-space dimension $m$, not asset-space dimension $n$. A book of 3,200 assets with 5 drivers is solved as a 5-dimensional PDE. The $n$-dependence enters only through a one-time $O(n)$ projection. This is not approximation — it's exact.
- **Unhedgeable geometry risk:** When the conditioning set changes (a "separator switch"), the resulting value jump is Kunita–Watanabe orthogonal to every continuously traded strategy. The market is **incomplete in the direction of its own geometry**. Pure rotations of $B$ (i.e., $B \mapsto BO$ for orthogonal $O$) are simultaneously undetectable (covariance is invariant), unhedgeable, and costly (~1.4% certainty-equivalent impact).
- **Practical implication for multi-strategy desks:** If your factor loadings are state-dependent — and they almost certainly are — then a static Markowitz allocation on conditional moments is missing a hedge component that the paper shows delivers +2.0% certainty-equivalent gain (8.1 standard errors) in a moving-manifold economy.

## Quick Insight: The Transport Connection

The manifold hedge has a clean geometric interpretation via the **transport connection** on the information manifold:

$$\widetilde{\omega}_t = E_t^\top G_t \dot{E}_t + \frac{1}{2}E_t^\top \dot{G}_t E_t$$

where $G(z) = B(z)^\top \Sigma_{\text{ret}}(z)^{-1}B(z)$ is the pullback metric. The first term captures **horizontal transport** (rotation of the factor basis), and the second captures **vertical transport** (breathing of the metric itself). When $B$ is constant, both vanish and you recover classical Merton. When $B$ moves, the horizontal term is the novel hedge — it's what standard intertemporal models, which treat state-dependence as exogenous, completely miss.

**Numerical result:** In the paper's synthetic economies, the $m$-dimensional backward recursion reproduces the full solver's Riccati trajectory to floating-point round-off ($\max|\Delta P| = 6.7 \times 10^{-15}$), and computation time is flat as $n$ scales from 10 to 3,200.

```python
# Sketch: projected Markowitz with manifold hedge
# B: loading matrix (n x m), Sigma_Z: driver cov (m x m)
# psi: predicted state motion (m,), mu: expected returns (n,)
# R: risk aversion scalar

import numpy as np

def causal_portfolio(B, Sigma_Z, psi, mu, rf, R, varsigma_sq):
    """Optimal policy: myopic + manifold hedge."""
    Lambda_Z = Sigma_Z  # driver innovation covariance
    Q = B @ Lambda_Z @ B.T + np.diag(varsigma_sq)  # conditional return cov
    M_C = np.linalg.solve(Q, B @ Lambda_Z @ B.T) / np.trace(
        np.linalg.solve(Q, B @ Lambda_Z @ B.T)
    )  # simplified projector
    # Myopic component
    w_myopic = (1/R) * np.linalg.solve(Q, mu - rf)
    # Manifold hedge component
    w_hedge = (1/R) * np.linalg.solve(Q, B @ Lambda_Z @ psi)
    return w_myopic, w_hedge
```

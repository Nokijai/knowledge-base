SPY's lag-1 autocorrelation is −0.081 with a z-score of −7.4 — one of the most statistically significant regularities in equity finance. Yet the standard variance-ratio test cannot tell you *why*: whether prices reverse direction, or whether large moves simply shrink. The Fourier-Residue Identity (FRI) solves this by decomposing autocorrelation into two independent, testable channels.

## What it is

The FRI, introduced by Victoria Portnaya (arXiv:2606.29591, June 2026), decomposes the return autocorrelation $\rho(m)$ into a **sign channel** (does the direction of today's return predict tomorrow's direction?) and a **magnitude channel** (does the size of today's return predict tomorrow's size?). The key insight: the standard autocorrelation function is a scalar that conflates both effects. The FRI uses the Fejér kernel to split them cleanly.

The core identity (Proposition 2.5 in the paper):

$$
\gamma_{A,k}(m) = \frac{\mathbb{E}[|r_t|^k \cdot |r_{t-m}|^k] - \mu_k^2}{\sigma_k^2}
$$

where $k=2$ gives the sign channel (directional persistence) and $k=4$ gives the magnitude channel (size persistence). Neither is a function of the other — a series with sign momentum but no magnitude clustering has $\text{VR}_2(q) > 1$, $\text{VR}_4(q) \approx 1$, and vice versa.

The paper also proves the **Fejér Identity** — that the classic Lo-MacKinlay variance-ratio test statistic is a spectral sum:

$$
\text{VR}(q) = 1 + 2\sum_{m=1}^{q-1} \left(1 - \frac{m}{q}\right) \rho(m)
$$

which gives the VR test a clean spectral interpretation: it tests whether the normalised power spectrum at zero frequency equals unity under Fejér smoothing.

## Why it matters

This is a practical tool for any quant trading desk. The FRI diagnosis has direct trading implications:

- If the **sign channel** is significant → directional reversal strategies (mean reversion) or trend-following (momentum) may work
- If only the **magnitude channel** is significant → volatility scaling, position sizing, and stop-loss logic matter more than direction prediction

The key empirical finding: **SPY's lag-1 autocorrelation is driven entirely by magnitude** (p < 10⁻¹² for magnitude, p = 0.11 for sign). A large move yesterday predicts a smaller move today *regardless of direction* — the fingerprint of bid-ask bounce and non-synchronous constituent staleness, not directional reversal. At lag 3, a separate directional reversal channel appears (p = 0.02) that is *invisible* to the scalar autocorrelation test.

The paper also provides a subsample diagnostic $R_N = G_{N/2} / G_N$ that tells practitioners whether a detected VR deviation is structural (persistent out-of-sample) or a finite-sample artefact.

## Quick example

Applied to SPY daily data (1993–2026):

| Test | Statistic | p-value | Interpretation |
|------|-----------|---------|----------------|
| Full autocorrelation | $z = -7.39$ | $<10^{-12}$ | Something is happening |
| FRI sign test | $z = -1.59$ | 0.11 | Direction is *not* predictable |
| FRI magnitude test | $z = -6.84$ | $<10^{-12}$ | Size is highly predictable |

A simple Python implementation:

```python
import numpy as np

def fri_sign_test(returns, max_lag=1):
    """FRI sign channel: test directional predictability alone."""
    n = len(returns)
    signs = np.sign(returns)
    # Probability same-sign at lag m
    p_same = np.mean(signs[:n-max_lag] == signs[max_lag:])
    rho_sign = 2 * p_same - 1
    # Asymptotic variance under H0
    se = np.sqrt(1 / n)
    z = rho_sign / se
    return rho_sign, z, 2 * (1 - norm.cdf(abs(z)))
```

The cross-asset results are equally striking: mean reversion is confined to exchange-traded equities and sovereign bonds; credit ETFs, commodities, FX, and crypto are indistinguishable from random walks on this test.

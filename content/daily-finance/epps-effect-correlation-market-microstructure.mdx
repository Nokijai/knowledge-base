**Two stocks that move together 60% of the time on a daily chart can appear almost uncorrelated at the one-second level — and if you do not know why, you will blow up a pairs trade.**

## What it is

The **Epps Effect**, first documented by economist T.W. Epps in 1979, is the empirical observation that the cross-correlation between the returns of two assets is a *decreasing function* of the measurement interval. At daily or weekly resolution, correlated stocks show typical Pearson correlations of 0.5–0.7. Zoom in to tick-by-tick data at millisecond resolution, and those same pairs often show a correlation below 0.05 — near zero.

This is not a statistical artefact. It is a *structural* property of how prices are formed in limit order books. A June 2026 paper by Angstmann and Gebbie (arXiv:2606.14182) now provides the first unified analytic account using coupled reaction–diffusion equations.

The effect arises from three distinct mechanisms:

1. **Asynchronous event clocks (subordination):** Two assets do not trade at the same instants. Sampling prices at regular calendar-time intervals often compares a "stale" price on one asset against a fresh one on another, injecting uncorrelated noise into the covariance estimator.
2. **Finite coupling response times:** Information that drives both assets spreads through the order books with a finite propagation delay. At time scales shorter than this delay, the two books appear decoupled.
3. **Combination effects:** The two mechanisms compound non-linearly, producing an Epps curve that is steeper than either alone.

In the reaction–diffusion model, realised correlation as a function of aggregation time $\tau$ takes the approximate closed form:

$$
\rho(\tau) \approx \rho_\infty \left(1 - e^{-\tau / \tau^*}\right)
$$

where $\rho_\infty$ is the "true" long-run correlation and $\tau^*$ is a characteristic time scale capturing both asynchrony and coupling latency.

## Why it matters / How it is used

**Pairs trading and stat-arb:** Strategies trading mean-reversion on co-integrated pairs estimate the hedge ratio from correlation. If the strategy executes at 1-minute bars but correlation was calibrated on daily data, the hedge ratio is systematically overstated — the position carries far more residual risk than expected.

**Portfolio optimisation:** Mean-variance optimisers fed with high-frequency correlation matrices will *underestimate* true correlations and therefore *overestimate* diversification benefits. The result is under-hedged positions — exactly the wrong outcome when correlations spike in a risk-off move.

**Options on baskets:** Basket options and correlation swaps require accurate cross-correlation inputs. Vol desks explicitly correct for the Epps effect by upward-adjusting HF correlation estimates before pricing.

**HFT signal design:** Recognising that correlation "turns on" above a certain $\tau^*$ threshold identifies the window within which cross-asset signals carry information — and avoids trading on spurious decorrelation below that threshold.

## Quick example / insight

Consider SPY and QQQ — historically $\rho_\infty \approx 0.92$ at daily frequency.

| Sampling interval | Observed correlation |
|---|---|
| 1 day | ~0.92 |
| 1 hour | ~0.80 |
| 5 minutes | ~0.55 |
| 1 minute | ~0.35 |
| 10 seconds | ~0.10 |

Same two instruments, same underlying comovement — only the measurement window changes. A naive HFT pairs strategy calibrated on tick data would compute a hedge ratio of essentially zero and hold *no hedge at all*.

**Python sketch — computing the Epps curve empirically:**

```python
import numpy as np

def epps_curve(prices_a, prices_b, intervals_seconds, base_freq=1):
    """
    Compute empirical Pearson correlation at multiple aggregation intervals.
    prices_a, prices_b: tick-level price series (same length)
    base_freq: ticks per second
    """
    correlations = {}
    for tau in intervals_seconds:
        k = int(tau * base_freq)
        n = len(prices_a) // k
        ret_a = np.diff(np.log(prices_a[:n*k].reshape(n, k)[:, -1]))
        ret_b = np.diff(np.log(prices_b[:n*k].reshape(n, k)[:, -1]))
        correlations[tau] = np.corrcoef(ret_a, ret_b)[0, 1]
    return correlations

# Example output tracing the Epps curve:
# {10: 0.08, 60: 0.35, 300: 0.55, 3600: 0.80, 86400: 0.92}
```

The inflection point of this curve is the characteristic coupling time $\tau^*$ — the *minimum safe bar size* for a pairs strategy on these two assets.

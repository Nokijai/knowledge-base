**Every institutional trader pays a hidden tax proportional to the square root of what they trade — and a 2025 study of 8 years of Tokyo Stock Exchange data just proved this law is universal and purely mechanical.**

## What it is

The **Square-Root Law of Market Impact** states that the average price impact $I$ of executing a large order (called a *metaorder*) scales as the square root of the traded volume $Q$:

$$I(Q) = Y \cdot \sigma \sqrt{\frac{Q}{V}}$$

where $\sigma$ is daily volatility, $V$ is daily traded volume, and $Y$ is a dimensionless prefactor of order 1. This means doubling your order size increases your market impact by only ~41%, not 100% — the relationship is concave (sublinear), not linear.

A **metaorder** is a large parent order sliced into many smaller child orders executed incrementally over time. The law applies to the *total* average price displacement caused by executing the whole metaorder.

## Why it matters

This law sits at the heart of every institutional execution desk and quant fund. It directly determines **transaction cost analysis (TCA)**, optimal execution algorithms (Almgren-Chriss, VWAP scheduling), and real-world P&L. If you ignore it and model impact as linear, you dramatically overestimate costs for small orders and underestimate them for large ones.

The "double" square-root paper by Bouchaud et al. (2025) added a crucial layer: the law holds at the *child order* level too, with a complementary **inverse square-root decay in time** as liquidity replenishes:

$$\text{child order impact}(t) \propto \sqrt{q} \cdot t^{-1/2}$$

The metaorder-level square-root law *emerges* from the convolution of these two effects — hence "double" square-root.

## Quick example

Say you manage a $1B equity fund and want to buy $10M of a stock with daily volume $100M and daily vol 1.5%.

$$I = 1.0 \times 1.5\% \times \sqrt{\frac{10M}{100M}} = 1.5\% \times 0.316 \approx 0.47\%$$

That's ~47 bps of slippage just from market impact — roughly $47,000 on that single trade. Now compare buying $40M of the same stock:

$$I = 1.5\% \times \sqrt{\frac{40M}{100M}} = 1.5\% \times 0.632 \approx 0.95\%$$

You quadrupled the size but only doubled the impact. The concavity is your friend — but it's still a real cost.

```python
import numpy as np

def market_impact(Q, V, sigma, Y=1.0):
    """
    Square-root market impact model.
    Returns impact in fractional price units (e.g. 0.005 = 50 bps).
    """
    return Y * sigma * np.sqrt(Q / V)

# Example
sigma = 0.015      # 1.5% daily vol
V = 100_000_000    # $100M daily volume

for Q in [1e6, 5e6, 10e6, 40e6]:
    impact_bps = market_impact(Q, V, sigma) * 10000
    print(f"Order ${Q/1e6:.0f}M → impact {impact_bps:.1f} bps")

# Order $1M → impact 15.0 bps
# Order $5M → impact 33.5 bps
# Order $10M → impact 47.4 bps
# Order $40M → impact 94.9 bps
```

## The mechanical debate — and why it matters philosophically

The classic view (Kyle 1985, informed trading models) says price impact exists because the market *infers information* from your order flow and adjusts accordingly. Big orders move prices because they signal private knowledge.

Bouchaud et al. (2025) showed this is largely wrong. Using trader-ID-resolved TSE data, they constructed **synthetic metaorders** — sequences of real market orders with trader identities *randomly shuffled*. These fake, informationally meaningless metaorders produced **identical square-root impact** as real ones.

Conclusion: price impact is primarily **mechanical** — it reflects liquidity supply and demand dynamics in the order book, not information revelation. Markets move because *someone is persistently buying*, not because the market has figured out *who* is buying.

This has a practical implication: **stealth is less important than you think**. The market doesn't need to identify you to react to your pressure. What matters is participation rate, not anonymity.

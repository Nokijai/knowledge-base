**Rough volatility models are notoriously difficult to distinguish from data—this paper shows you don't need to calibrate parameters at all. Just read the geometry of the path.**

## What It Is

**Path signatures** provide a mathematical tool for encoding the temporal structure of a time series as a series of iterated integrals. The signature $S(X)_{s,t}$ of a path $X$ captures all information about the path's shape:

$$S(X)_{s,t}=\Big(1,\ \int_{s}^{t}dX,\ \int_{s<u_{1}<u_{2}<t}dX_{u_{1}}\otimes dX_{u_{2}},\ \dots\Big)$$

This paper (Burés and De Santiago, July 2026) demonstrates that **truncated signatures at depth 4** (only 31 features) are sufficient to classify volatility model classes with near-perfect accuracy. The method works by feeding these signature features into a gradient boosting classifier (XGBoost) that learns to distinguish between:

- **Heston model** (classical stochastic volatility): $dv_{t}=\kappa(\theta-v_{t})\,dt+\nu\sqrt{v_{t}}\,dW_{t}$
- **Rough Bergomi model** (rough volatility): $v_{t}=v_{0}\exp\left(\eta W_{t}^{H}-\tfrac{1}{2}\eta^{2}t^{2H}\right)$ with $H<1/2$
- **Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process** (benchmark)

The key insight: **model classes leave distinct geometric fingerprints** in their sample paths, and signatures capture these fingerprints in a universal, parameter-free representation.

## Why It Matters

Traditional volatility model calibration faces a fundamental problem: **you need to specify the model structure before you can calibrate it.** If you fit a Heston model to data generated by a rough volatility process, you will get parameters—but they will not make sense, and you will not know why.

This framework solves the **model identification problem**:

1. **Model selection before calibration:** Instead of blindly fitting a parametric model, you can first identify which *class* of model generated your data, then calibrate within that class.

2. **Robustness to parameter uncertainty:** The classifier maintains 84-99% accuracy even when each path is simulated with randomly sampled parameters—proving it learns structural properties, not parameter-specific quirks.

3. **Rough volatility detection:** The method distinguishes between rough volatility models with Hurst parameters $H=0.05, 0.15, 0.25, 0.35$—parameters so close that traditional estimation methods struggle to separate them.

4. **Real trading desk applications:** 
   - **Pricing desk:** Identify whether your volatility surface exhibits rough or classical dynamics before choosing a pricing model
   - **Risk management:** Detect regime changes in volatility behavior (rough to classical or vice versa)
   - **Model validation:** Verify that your production model matches the *type* of dynamics present in market data

## Quick Example: The Power of Four Levels

Consider the classification task: **Heston vs. Rough Bergomi with $H=0.1$ vs. Rough Bergomi with $H=0.3$**

With fixed parameters, the classifier achieves **99.96% accuracy** on 200,000 out-of-sample paths. Only 8 paths are misclassified—all of them borderline cases where rough Bergomi with $H=0.6$ (near-classical) slightly resembles Heston.

Under **parameter uncertainty** (each path drawn with random parameters from broad distributions):
- Heston: **100% correctly identified**
- Rough Bergomi $H=0.1$: **99.2% accuracy**  
- Rough Bergomi $H=0.2$: **97.8% accuracy** (hardest, as it sits between classes)
- Rough Bergomi $H=0.3$: **98.5% accuracy**

**Feature importance analysis** reveals the secret: signature terms of **order 3 and 4** dominate classification. The most powerful feature is a fourth-order iterated integral, which captures nonlinear temporal interactions invisible to simpler methods.

## The Deeper Insight: Geometry Over Parameters

The paper's title captures the philosophical shift: **"identification of volatility models from path geometry."** Rather than asking "what parameters fit this data best?", it asks "what *kind of system* generated this data?"

This matters because rough volatility research (Gatheral, Jaisson and Rosenbaum 2018) showed that volatility is rougher than Brownian motion (typical $H \approx 0.1-0.2$). But once you accept roughness, you face a new problem: **how rough?** Traditional Hurst parameter estimation is noisy and inconsistent across methods. This signature approach bypasses the estimation problem entirely and goes straight to classification.

The fact that **depth-4 signatures suffice** is remarkable. Volatility paths are typically thousands of points long, yet 31 signature features contain all the discriminative information. Higher-order terms (depth 5+) provide only marginal gains. This suggests the essential geometry of volatility dynamics is captured by low-order temporal interactions—exactly what you would expect if the underlying process is fundamentally simple (like fractional Brownian motion with added mean reversion).

## Practical Limitations

The method requires **volatility paths as input**—not prices, not returns, but *volatility* (typically realized volatility or variance swap estimates). In practice, you would need to construct a volatility proxy first, introducing estimation noise not present in the paper's controlled simulations.

For **real-time trading applications**, the approach may need adaptation: the paper classifies entire paths, but a trading desk might need to identify model regimes from rolling windows, potentially with overlapping or streaming data.
